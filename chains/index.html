What a Chain actually is

Each week, an email arrives. Not a newsletter. Not a tip sheet. A lesson — the kind that assumes you're serious and treats you accordingly.

The lessons are built around Wild Magic, a set of writing techniques centered on trust, wonder, and voice discovery. The goal is never to make you write like someone else. It's to help you figure out what you already know — and how to get it onto the page.

Anyone can write. Not everyone can trust their voice. That's what the Chains work on.

How they work

You purchase a Chain. Eight weeks later, you've covered the territory. Each email includes the lesson itself, exercises if you want them, and enough room to disagree.

You can take one Chain or all eight. You can take them in order or by what feels most urgent. The Life and the Craft are good starting places if you're not sure — but start where you actually are, not where you think you're supposed to be.

Who's writing these

I'm Jacob Clifton. For fifteen years I was a flagship writer at Television Without Pity — one of the most widely-read TV criticism sites of its era, at its peak about a million readers a week. I've written for Tor.com, Vulture, BuzzFeed, the Austin Chronicle. I've been teaching writers for years through A Rough Trade Writing School, through Patreon, through one-on-one work.

The 8 Chains are built around what I've actually learned — about writing, about being a writer, about what it costs and what it gives back. They're not based on a theory. They're based on doing it, badly and well, for a very long time.

The thing that makes you awesome is the thing that makes you suck. The Chains exist to help you figure out which is which — and what to do about either one.
